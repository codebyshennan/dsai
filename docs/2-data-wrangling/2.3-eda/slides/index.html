What is EDA and Why It Comes First

  • EDA is a structured investigation of your data before modelling or reporting
  • Coined by John Tukey (1977): let the data speak before fitting a model
  • Catch wrong units, skewed distributions, leaky features, silent missingness early
  • Builds intuition about the domain — essential for choosing the right model
  • EDA saves hours of debugging and gives stakeholders confidence in your numbers
  • Mindset: be a detective, not a statistician — ask questions, then measure

The EDA Workflow

  • Step 1 — Load & inspect: shape, dtypes, memory, first/last rows
  • Step 2 — Univariate analysis: distribution of each column individually
  • Step 3 — Summary statistics: describe(), value_counts(), missing counts
  • Step 4 — Missing data: patterns, heatmaps, decide MCAR / MAR / MNAR
  • Step 5 — Outlier detection: visual (box plot) + statistical (IQR, z-score)
  • Step 6 — Bivariate analysis: scatter plots, correlation matrices
  • Step 7 — Categorical relationships: bar charts, cross-tabs, chi-square
  • Step 8 — Time series EDA: trends, seasonality, rolling statistics
  • Step 9 — Document findings: hypotheses to test, red flags to address

Univariate Analysis

Understanding one variable at a time

Data Science & AI | Skills Union × Localized

Histograms and Distribution Shape

  • A histogram bins values and counts frequency — first visual check for any numeric column
  • Add a KDE curve (sns.histplot(kde=True)) to see the smooth density estimate
  • Normal (bell): mean ≈ median, symmetric tails — parametric methods safe
  • Right-skewed: long right tail, mean > median — common in revenue, income
  • Left-skewed: long left tail, mean < median — common in ratings, scores
  • Bimodal: two peaks — often a hidden sub-population or mixing of groups
  • Rule of thumb: |skewness| > 1 is significant and usually needs a transform
  • df['col'].hist(bins=30) or sns.histplot(df['col'], bins=30, kde=True)
Data Science & Analytics | Skills Union

Box Plots and Skewness

  • A box plot shows Q1, median, Q3 and the IQR fence in one glance
  • Box spans Q1 to Q3 (middle 50 % of data); line inside is the median
  • Whiskers extend to 1.5 × IQR; points beyond are drawn as individual dots
  • Skewness: $\gamma_1 = \frac{m_3}{m_2^{3/2}}$ — positive means right tail is longer
  • Kurtosis: $\gamma_2 = \frac{m_4}{m_2^2} - 3$ — heavy tails have positive excess kurtosis
  • When median is close to Q1, the distribution is right-skewed (long upper whisker)
  • sns.boxplot(y=df['col']) or df.boxplot(column='col')

Summary Statistics

Numbers that summarise a column

Central Tendency and Spread

  • Mean: $\bar{x} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n x_i$ — best for symmetric distributions, sensitive to outliers
  • Median: middle value when sorted — robust, better for skewed data
  • Mode: most frequent value — use for categorical and discrete data
  • Std: $\sigma = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum (x_i - \bar{x})^2}$ — same units as the data
  • IQR: $Q_3 - Q_1$ — robust spread measure used in outlier fences
  • When mean >> median: suspect right skew and outliers pulling the mean up
  • Rule: use median + IQR for skewed data, mean + std for symmetric data

Using describe() and value_counts()

  • df.describe() → count, mean, std, min, 25 %, 50 %, 75 %, max for all numeric columns
  • df.describe(include='all') → also includes top/freq/unique for categoricals
  • df['col'].value_counts() → frequency table for categorical or discrete columns
  • df['col'].value_counts(normalize=True) → proportion instead of count
  • df.info() → dtypes and non-null counts in one call — first sanity check
  • Compare min/max to domain expectations — spot impossible values early
  • Check count vs len(df) per column to surface missing data quickly

Outlier Detection During EDA

Visual and statistical methods

Data Science & AI | Skills Union × Localized

Visual Outlier Detection

  • Box plot: points beyond 1.5 × IQR fences are flagged as potential outliers
  • Histogram: isolated bars at extremes indicate outliers
  • Scatter plot: isolated points far from the cloud are visible immediately
  • Strip / swarm plot: shows individual points — useful for small datasets
  • Always plot before deciding — some 'outliers' are correct extreme values (e.g. billionaires in income data)
  • Plot with and without outliers to understand their impact on the distribution
Data Science & Analytics | Skills Union

Statistical Outlier Detection

  • IQR method: outlier if value < Q1 − 1.5 × IQR or > Q3 + 1.5 × IQR
  • Z-score method: outlier if |z| > 3, where $z = \frac{x - \bar{x}}{\sigma}$
  • IQR is more robust (not affected by outliers themselves); z-score assumes near-normality
  • Use z-score only when data is roughly symmetric; prefer IQR for skewed data
  • Decision options: keep, cap (winsorize), transform (log), or remove — justify each choice
  • Document every outlier decision — reviewers and future-you will thank you

Missing Data Patterns

Understand before imputing

Data Science & AI | Skills Union × Localized

Missing Data Analysis and Heatmaps

  • MCAR (Missing Completely At Random): no pattern — safe to drop or impute
  • MAR (Missing At Random): missingness depends on other columns — impute carefully
  • MNAR (Missing Not At Random): missingness depends on the missing value itself — hardest case
  • df.isnull().sum() → count per column; / len(df) * 100 for percentage
  • Missingness heatmap: sns.heatmap(df.isnull(), cbar=False) — spots row-level patterns
  • Correlation of missingness: df.isnull().corr() — finds columns that go missing together
  • Never blindly dropna() — always profile the pattern first to avoid silent bias

Bivariate Analysis

Exploring relationships between two variables

Data Science & AI | Skills Union × Localized

Scatter Plots and Correlation Matrices

  • Scatter plot: plot two numeric columns against each other to see linear or non-linear patterns
  • Add a regression line (sns.regplot) to estimate the direction and slope at a glance
  • Pearson r: $r = \frac{\sum (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{\sqrt{\sum (x_i-\bar{x})^2}\sqrt{\sum(y_i-\bar{y})^2}}$ — linear only, −1 to 1
  • Spearman ρ: rank-based, handles monotonic non-linear relationships
  • Correlation heatmap: sns.heatmap(df.corr(), annot=True, cmap='coolwarm') — overview of all pairs
  • r > 0.7 or < −0.7 is usually considered strong; always visualise to confirm
  • Correlation ≠ causation — lurking variables and reverse causality are common

Categorical Relationships

Bar charts, cross-tabs, and group comparisons

Data Science & AI | Skills Union × Localized

Bar Charts and Cross-Tabulations

  • Bar chart: df['col'].value_counts().plot(kind='bar') — frequency of each category
  • Grouped bar chart: compare one numeric metric across categories side by side
  • Stacked bar chart: shows proportions within each category
  • Cross-tabulation: pd.crosstab(df['A'], df['B']) — joint frequency table for two categoricals
  • Chi-square test: $\chi^2 = \sum \frac{(O - E)^2}{E}$ — test independence between two categorical columns
  • Cramér's V: $V = \sqrt{\frac{\chi^2}{n \cdot \min(r-1, c-1)}}$ — effect size, 0 (no association) to 1 (perfect)
  • Box plot by category: sns.boxplot(x='category', y='numeric', data=df) — compare distributions

Time Series EDA

Exploring temporal patterns

Data Science & AI | Skills Union × Localized

Trends, Seasonality, and Rolling Statistics

  • Parse dates first: pd.to_datetime(df['date']) → set as index for resampling
  • Line plot: df.set_index('date')['value'].plot() — first check for trends and spikes
  • Decomposition: separate Trend ($T_t$), Seasonality ($S_t$), Residuals ($R_t$)
  • Additive: $Y_t = T_t + S_t + R_t$ — use when seasonal amplitude is constant
  • Multiplicative: $Y_t = T_t \times S_t \times R_t$ — use when amplitude grows with the trend
  • Simple moving average: $MA_t = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=0}^{n-1} Y_{t-i}$ — smooths noise, reveals trend
  • EMA: $EMA_t = \alpha Y_t + (1-\alpha)EMA_{t-1}$ — reacts faster to recent changes
  • df.resample('M').mean() — aggregate to monthly; rolling(7).mean() — 7-day smoothing

Time Series Anomaly Detection

  • Rolling z-score: flag points beyond rolling_mean ± 3 × rolling_std
  • rolling_mean = series.rolling(window=30).mean()
  • rolling_std = series.rolling(window=30).std()
  • Anomaly = value outside rolling_mean ± threshold × rolling_std
  • Seasonal anomaly: compare same period across years (e.g. December week vs prior Decembers)
  • ACF plot: autocorrelation function reveals the dominant seasonal period
  • Plot anomalies as red markers on the original line chart for immediate visibility

EDA Workflow Summary

A repeatable checklist

Data Science & AI | Skills Union × Localized

EDA Checklist Before Modelling

  • Load: confirm shape, dtypes, memory; scan first and last rows
  • Profile: describe(include='all'), info(), missing counts per column
  • Univariate: histogram + box plot for every numeric; bar chart for every categorical
  • Outliers: IQR fence or z-score; visualise and decide keep / cap / remove
  • Missing data: heatmap, correlation of missingness, classify MCAR / MAR / MNAR
  • Bivariate: correlation heatmap, scatter plots for key pairs, cross-tabs for categoricals
  • Temporal (if applicable): line plot, decomposition, rolling stats, anomaly scan
  • Document: write down hypotheses, red flags, and data quality decisions before modelling
